ABSTRACT
Sentiment analysis (SA) of online reviews is pivotal for e-commerce platforms yet challenges such as massive 
user-generated content volumes and class imbalances hinder accurate multiclass predictions and model inter -
pretability. This study introduces a novel explainable ensemble learning framework for multiclass SA (positive, 
neutral, negative) across three Amazon product domains: appliances, groceries, and clothing. The framework 
integrates diverse supervised classifiers in a stacking ensemble, with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) 
innovatively employed not only to elucidate feature contributions but also to rank and interpret the individual 
impacts of base classifiers on ensemble predictions, a pioneering application in domain-specific SA, as it enables 
global insights into model dynamics and base model selection, addressing gaps in prior studies that relied on 
local explanations like LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations). Evaluated using imbalance- 
sensitive metrics (weighted/macro F1-score, Matthews Correlation Coefficient, Cohen ’ s Kappa, Geometric 
Mean), the ensemble surpasses individual classifiers and demonstrates higher macro F1 and G-Mean than the 
transformer-based ALBERT model, while ALBERT excels in weighted F1, MCC, and Cohen's Kappa. Extra Trees 
notably excelled in the G-Mean for minority classes. SHAP analysis uncovers domain-specific drivers and base 
model roles, enhancing transparency. The results underscore the framework ’ s efficacy in delivering robust 
performance and actionable insights for trust modelling, automated analytics, and personalized recommenda -
tions. This work lays the groundwork for extensions to low-resource domains, multimodal data, and finer rating 
scales, advancing interpretable SA in e-commerce.
1. Introduction
In the rapidly evolving digital landscape, online shopping has gained 
significant traction in recent years due to increased internet penetration 
and advancements in mobile technologies ( Shetty et al., 2024 ). As a 
result, customers frequently share their experiences through online 
product reviews, contributing to a massive volume of user-generated 
reviews ( Hashmi & Yayilgan, 2024 ). Analyzing these reviews can aid 
businesses align offerings with expectations, identify market advan -
tages, and improve products by understanding customer needs and pain 
points ( Ray et al., 2024 ). However, the overwhelming volume of such 
reviews presents a significant challenge for manual analysis, which is 
often inefficient and inconsistent ( Maruthuperumal, 2025 ). Thus, 
e-commerce platforms have increasingly adopted Artificial Intelligence 
(AI) solutions to efficiently process large datasets, categorize feedback, 
and uncover trends for strategic decision-making ( Nnanna-Ohuonu, 
2025 ).
In particular, Sentiment Analysis (SA), a branch of Natural Language 
Processing (NLP), is widely used to interpret customer sentiments from 
large volumes of textual data ( Gupta et al., 2021 ). By incorporating SA 
into customer feedback analysis processes, manufacturers can perform 
the transformation of qualitative data into quantitative, measurable 
results for seamless identification of prevailing sentiment patterns and 
underlying causes driving consumer opinions ( Mara et al., 2025 ). 
Compared to manual methods, automated sentiment classification offers 
efficiency and consistency while reducing human bias in interpretation 
( Rana et al., 2022 ). However, despite recent progress, SA systems face 
key limitations, especially in the areas of sentiment granularity, domain 
adaptability, and model transparency.
Most existing studies primarily focus on binary sentiment classifi -
cation (positive, negative), often overlooking the nuanced nature of 
multiclass sentiment (positive, neutral, negative) ( Liu, 2022 ). However, 
customer feedback often isn ’ t strictly positive or negative. For instance, 
neutral sentiments (e.g., the shirt fits fine, but the color is average) are

nuances enables better product improvements, customer service prior -

To address these limitations, this study proposes an explainable 
ensemble ML framework for multi-domain, multiclass SA in Amazon 
product reviews. The study makes the following contributions: 
1. It proposes a multi-domain, multiclass ensemble learning framework 
for sentiment classification of Amazon product reviews, covering 
appliances, groceries, and clothing.
2. It incorporates explainable AI techniques, specifically SHapley Ad -
ditive exPlanations (SHAP), not only to explain feature contributions

state-of-the-art transformer model, A Light BERT (ALBERT), enabling a 
rigorous comparison between traditional ensemble methods and mod -
ern deep language models.

experimental results and model evaluations. Section 5 discusses findings 
and implications. Finally, Section 6 concludes the study and outlines 
directions for future research.
2. Literature review and background
2.1. Product domain and classification
Existing studies on SA demonstrate considerable variation in their 
selection of product domains and the granularity of sentiment classifi -
cation, reflecting diverse research objectives and methodological ap -
proaches. This heterogeneity underscores both the progress and 
persistent limitations in the field, particularly regarding multi-domain, 
multiclass frameworks and transparent data reporting. Lin (2020)
focused on SA of women’s clothing reviews, employing a binary classi -
fication scheme (recommended vs. not recommended. Similarly, Noor 
and Islam (2019) and Shetty et al. (2024) examined Amazon women’s 
clothing reviews, applying binary classification, with datasets contain -
ing over 50,000 and 23,486 reviews, respectively.
Gupta et al. (2021) expanded the scope by examining Amazon re -
views across clothing, shoes, and jewelry, applying binary classification 
with a dataset exceeding 700,000 reviews. The broader domain 
coverage represented an advancement toward multi-domain SA; how -
ever, the binary framework failed to address the nuanced sentiment 
levels essential for comprehensive customer insights. Mahmud et al. 
(2023) ventured into multiclass sentiment classification for women’s 
clothing reviews, categorizing sentiments into multiple classes (e.g., 
positive, neutral, negative). Despite this progress, the lack of specified 
dataset details (e.g., source, size) impedes reproducibility and general -
izability, highlighting a critical gap in transparent data reporting that 
hinders cross-study validation.
Hashmi and Yayilgan (2024) employed a hybrid approach with 
product context-aware learning. They analyzed over 500,000 Amazon 
reviews from diverse domains, including mobile electronics, security 
software, personal care appliances, and major appliances. Utilizing 
multiclass classification, the authors marked a step toward addressing 
the limitations of binary, single-domain studies. Ray et al. (2024) carried 
out a large-scale study with over 6 million Amazon reviews, focusing on 
appliances and using a multiclass classification approach. The extensive 
dataset size underscores the potential for robust multiclass SA, though 
the lack of cross-domain analysis limits its scope.
Radovilsky and Hegde (2024) explored grocery reviews from 
Amazon, Walmart, and Instacart, applying binary classification to a 
dataset of over 700,000 reviews. The multi-source approach enriched 
data diversity, but the binary classification restricted the inclusion of 
neutral sentiments, a key aspect of customer feedback. Along similar 
lines, Rana et al. (2022) and Arnav et al. (2024) analyzed Amazon re -
views on groceries using binary classification on large datasets (over 
700,000 and 500,000 reviews).
2.2. Ensemble machine learning techniques
Ensemble models in ML combine multiple base learners or learning 
processes to enhance predictive performance, robustness, and general -
ization compared to individual models. By leveraging the strengths of 
diverse models or iterative learning strategies, ensemble methods miti -
gate weaknesses such as overfitting, bias, and variance, making them 
particularly effective for complex tasks like multiclass sentiment anal -
ysis. The primary ensemble strategies, bagging, boosting, and stacking, 
employ distinct approaches to achieve these improvements, as follows: 
1. Bagging reduces variance by training multiple base models inde -
pendently on random subsets of the training data and aggregating 
their predictions, typically via majority voting for classification. 
Notable implementations include the Random Forest (RF) algorithm, 
which combines multiple decision trees with feature randomness to 
improve accuracy.
2. Boosting sequentially trains base models, with each model focusing 
on correcting the errors of its predecessors by assigning higher 
weights to misclassified instances. Notable implementations include 
XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost, which offer high accuracy and 
speed through optimizations like parallel processing and histogram- 
based algorithms. The foundational algorithm is AdaBoost, which 
adaptively assigns weights to misclassified samples.
3. Stacking, also called stacked generalization, is an ensemble learning 
technique that combines the predictions of multiple diverse base 
models using a meta-learner to produce a final, optimized prediction. 
This method aims to leverage the individual strengths of various 
algorithms and reduce potential weaknesses or biases of any single 
model.
These ensemble strategies enhance predictive accuracy and robust -
ness by addressing different aspects of model performance. For instance, 
bagging stabilizes predictions, boosting refines accuracy through itera -
tive learning, and stacking capitalizes on model diversity. Noor and

in specific contexts.
2.3. Explainable AI methods and applications
The complexity of high-performing ML models, particularly 
ensemble methods like RF, XGBoost, and stacking ensembles, often 
renders their decision-making processes opaque, posing challenges for 
understanding and trusting their predictions. In SA, where models must 
accurately classify nuanced sentiments (positive, neutral, negative) 
across diverse domains such as appliances, groceries, or clothing, this 
lack of interpretability can hinder practical adoption. Explainable AI 
(XAI) has emerged as a critical field to address this issue by developing 
methods to enhance the transparency and interpretability of complex 
ML models, thereby fostering trust and reliability in their decisions ( Tjoa 
& Guan, 2020 ). While numerous SA studies, such as those by Noor and 
Islam (2019) , Shetty et al. (2024) , Arnav et al. (2024) , Radovilsky and 
Hegde (2024) , and Lin (2020) , leverage advanced ML and ensemble 
models to achieve high accuracy (e.g., 98 % with LightGBM by Lin 
(2020) , 94 % with CatBoost by Hashmi and Yayilgan (2024) ), they often 
overlook explainability, which is a significant gap, as understanding the 
underlying mechanisms driving model predictions is essential for vali -
dating their reliability, identifying biases, and ensuring actionable in -
sights for stakeholders, particularly in multiclass SA where neutral 
sentiments add complexity.
Two widely adopted, model-agnostic XAI techniques for interpreting 
these models are LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explana -
tions) and SHAP ( Slack et al., 2020 ). LIME explains individual pre -
dictions by approximating the complex model locally with a simpler, 
interpretable model (e.g., linear regression) around the instance of in -
terest. It perturbs the input data and observes how predictions change, 
highlighting features (e.g., words in a review) that most influence the 
outcome. In SA, LIME can reveal which keywords or phrases drive a 
model to classify a review as positive, neutral, or negative. On the other 
hand, SHAP leverages game theory to assign importance scores (Shapley 
values) to each feature, quantifying its contribution to the model’s 
prediction. Unlike LIME, SHAP provides both local (instance-specific) 
and global (model-wide) explanations, offering a comprehensive view of 
feature importance across datasets. Hashmi and Yayilgan (2024) applied 
LIME to their FastXCatStack model, a stacking ensemble that integrated 
ML models, including XGBoost and CatBoost, for SA of personal care 
appliance reviews. LIME provided interpretable explanations for indi -
vidual prediction outcomes, elucidating why specific reviews were 
classified as positive, neutral, or negative.
2.4. Synthesis and research gap
While SA research has made significant strides in leveraging 
ensemble models to classify customer opinions, several critical gaps 
persist. These gaps, outlined below, underscore the need for more 
comprehensive approaches that address multi-domain, multiclass 
sentiment classification, robust ensemble model evaluation, and 
enhanced model interpretability. 
1. Limited focus on multi-domain, multiclass sentiment analysis 
The studies reveal a spectrum of approaches, with dataset sizes 
ranging from 23,486 to over 6 million reviews and domains spanning 
clothing, groceries, appliances, and electronics. The shift toward 
multiclass classification in Hashmi and Yayilgan (2024) , Ray et al. 
(2024) , and Mahmud et al. (2023) addresses the gap in capturing 
neutral sentiments, a critical need for holistic SA. However, the 
majority of studies primarily focus on single-domain datasets or bi -
nary sentiment classification, overlooking the nuanced nature of 
multiclass sentiment across diverse product categories. For instance, 
domains like appliances, groceries, and clothing exhibit distinct 
linguistic patterns and sentiment indicators, which require tailored 
models to capture domain-specific nuances. Thus, multi-domain, 
multiclass SA remains largely underexplored, limiting insights into 
customer feedback that spans everyday product categories and in -
cludes neutral sentiments, which are critical for a holistic under -
standing of user opinions.
2. Inconsistent benefits of ensemble models 
Ensemble models have demonstrated improved predictive per -
formance in SA, with notable results from Hashmi, Ehtesham (2024) , 
Lin (2020) , and Arnav et al. (2024) . However, the advantages of 
ensemble methods like bagging and boosting over traditional 
models, such as SVMs, are inconsistently reported. For instance, 
Hashmi and Yayilgan (2024) found that classical models occasion -
ally matched or outperformed ensembles in certain datasets. 
Furthermore, advanced techniques such as stacking combined with 
classical ML models have received limited attention. Effectively 
developed, these methods can enhance model robustness across do -
mains, yet their efficacy in multi-domain SA remains understudied, 
leaving a gap in understanding how to optimally integrate diverse 
models for improved generalization.
3. Insufficient focus on model interpretability 
The interpretability of ensembles is frequently overlooked in SA 
research, despite the critical need to understand decision-making 
processes to ensure reliability and trust. While local explanation 
techniques like LIME provide insights into individual predictions, 
they rarely offer a global perspective on overall model behavior or 
the contributions of base models within ensembles. Techniques like 
SHAP, which provide both local and global interpretability by 
quantifying feature contributions, remain underexplored in SA. This 
gap limits the ability to comprehensively understand model dy -
namics, identify biases, and refine ensemble models for multi- 
domain, multiclass tasks.
2.5. Significance and novelty
To address these gaps, particularly the limited focus on multi- 
domain, multiclass classification and the lack of comprehensive inter -
pretability for complex ensemble models, this study proposes a novel 
application of SHAP in the context of a stacking ensemble for domain- 
specific sentiment classification. Specifically, our approach leverages 
SHAP to achieve two objectives: (1) to explain the contributions of in -
dividual features (e.g., keywords, phrases) to sentiment predictions

3. Methodology
To address the research objectives, a methodology framework was 
adopted. Fig. 1 presents an overview of the key steps followed in this 
study, including data collection, preprocessing, modeling, performance 
evaluation and comparison, ensemble base model ranking, and 
explainability.
3.1. Data collection and description
The study uses the 2023 McAuley Lab Amazon Reviews release, 
including Appliances, Groceries & Gourmet Food, and Clothing, Shoes & 
jewelry categories, obtained from the University of California, San Diego 
(UCSD) ( Hou et al., 2024 ). The datasets were retrieved in JSON format 
(retrieved March 2025) and contain over one million product reviews 
each. Each dataset includes 11 fields: rating, title, text, images, asin, 
parent_asin, user_id, timestamp, helpful_vote, and verified_purchase, 
with reviews spanning from the early 2000s to 2023.
3.2. Preprocessing pipeline
Naturally, sentiment datasets contain noisy and unstructured user 
reviews. Data preprocessing is crucial for ensuring data quality and 
consistency before the modeling phase. In our case, the initial phase 
involved standard data cleaning operations, including converting all 
texts to lowercase, the removal of duplicate entries, handling missing 
values, stripping HTML tags (with BeautifulSoup4 v4.12.3 ), and elimi -
nating special characters and numbers. Non-English reviews were 
filtered out using the Langdetect library v1.0.9 , retaining only reviews 
with predicted 'en' language. To retain only relevant and recent data, 
reviews older than 10 years were excluded, keeping records from 2013 
to 2023. Emojis were converted into text to preserve the original 
emotional tone of user feedback using the Emoji library v2.14.1 . Com -
mon contraction words were also expanded (e.g., “ don ’ t ” to “ do not ” ), as 
they often carry sentiment cues and can lead to misinterpretation of 
sentiments if not properly handled.
Further preprocessing included tokenization with Spacy en_cor -
e_web_sm v3.8.2 , removing stop words to eliminate frequently occurring 
but semantically weak terms. However, negation words such as “ not, ” 
“ no, ” and “ never ” were carefully retained to avoid sentiment distortion.

3.3. Feature engineering
Feature engineering was employed to enhance model performance 
by extracting and transforming relevant features from the original var -
iables. In addition to TF-IDF, we experimented with modern embeddings 
using Sentence-BERT ( v3.0.1 ), including the MPNet and All-MiniLM-L6 
variants, across all domains. Despite their theoretical advantages in 
capturing semantic nuances, TF-IDF consistently achieved higher per -
formance in our datasets, likely due to domain-specific sparsity and the 
nature of Amazon reviews. Therefore, TF-IDF remains the primary 
feature representation in this study. We applied a combination of 
unigrams and bigrams. Using n-grams, especially bigrams, ensures that 
negation patterns such as “ not good ” , “ never buy ” , “ no value ” , etc., are 
correctly captured. For dimensionality reduction and faster model 
training, a selection of 100,000 maximum features was chosen, ensuring 
that only the top 100,000 most important terms are prioritized.

3.4. Experimental setup
All experiments were conducted on a local machine running Ubuntu 
Linux operating system, equipped with an Intel Core i5 processor, 32GB 
RAM, and an Intel Iris Xe Graphics GPU, running Python 3.12 in a Conda 
environment. Key libraries used include Numpy ( v2.2.2 ), Pandas 
( v2.2.3 ) for data manipulation and preprocessing, Matplotlib ( 3.10.0 ) 
and Seaborn ( 0.13.2 ) for visualization, Scikit-learn ( v1.6.1 ), XGBoost 
( v2.1.4 ), and LightGBM ( v4.5.0 ) model training, and SHAP ( v0.47.2 ) for 
model explainability. For initial model benchmarking, the LazyPredict 
( v0.2.13 ) library was used to evaluate the performance of a wide range 
of ML models. This process was performed on a 20,000-record subset in 
each domain. The top 10 performing models were selected for further 
cross-validation and model training. A fixed random seed of 42 was used 
throughout the entire pipeline to ensure reproducibility.
Each domain consisted of 100,000 records, which were sampled 
through stratified sampling without replacement from larger raw data -
sets to help preserve class distribution. The data was split into 80 % and 
20 % for model training and testing. Five-fold cross-validation was 
performed on each domain model set, which were then used as stacking 
ensemble base models. All experiments were conducted on an imbal -
anced multiclass dataset (negative, neutral, and positive).
3.5. Stacking ensemble learning
Ensemble learning combines multiple classifiers to improve predic -
tion accuracy. Stacking involves training diverse base learners on the 
full dataset, then using their predicted probabilities as input features for 
a meta-model. This approach leverages the complementary strengths of 
heterogeneous base models, often resulting in better performance than 
any individual classifier ( Rani & Gill, 2020 ). Fig. 3 illustrates the ar -
chitecture of the stacking model.
Multiple individual models (also known as base learners or Level-1 
models) generate predictions on a held-out validation data or using 
cross-validation. This is crucial to prevent data leakage and ensure the 
meta-motel learns from out-of-sample predictions. The predictions are 
then used as training data for the meta-model (also known as Level-2 
models or meta-learner). The meta-model learns how to optimally 
combine the predictions of base models to produce a final prediction. 
The meta-model can be any type of ML algorithm, such as a linear 
model, a tree-based model, or even another ensemble. The selection of 
the most effective domain-specific meta-model for this study is discussed 
in Section 4 .

Rather than applying oversampling techniques like the synthetic mi -

natural imbalance ensures that the testing process reflects real-world 
data distributions, thereby enabling a rigorous evaluation of the 
model's ability to generalize across different sentiment classes ( Suhaeni 
& Yong, 2024 ). However, to ensure a fair recognition of all classes, we 
incorporated a cost-sensitive learning approach, which adjusts 
misclassification penalties to improve the model's sensitivity to minority 
classes.
As shown in Fig. 4 , our datasets contain a higher proportion of 
positive reviews compared to negative and neutral ones. This imbalance 
necessitates careful selection of evaluation metrics, as conventional 
measures such as accuracy may not adequately reflect performance on 
the minority classes, potentially leading to misleading model assessment 
( Wegier & Ksieniewicz, 2020 ). To ensure a fair assessment across all 
classes, the following evaluation metrics are employed.

MCC is regarded as a comprehensive metric, particularly effective for 
evaluating models on an imbalanced dataset. Its value ranges from 
positive one, indicating perfect predictions, to negative one, represent -

3.7. Explainable ensemble learning framework
Zhong et al. (2022) highlight several issues associated with the lack 
of explainability in ML models, noting that models with high predictive 
accuracy are often more difficult to explain. Thus, the lack of explain -
ability limits the use of such models in scientific tasks, including 
uncovering hidden causal relationships, acquiring useful knowledge, 
and developing scientific hypotheses, leading to skepticism due to their 
black-box nature ( Ngwenya et al., 2025a , 2025b ). This issue of 
non-explainability extends to SA, particularly in multi-domain studies 
like ours, where understanding why a model predicts a given sentiment 
is critical for identifying domain-specific features that drive accurate 
sentiment prediction, foster trust, and support actionable insights. SHAP 
is a model-agnostic, post-hoc explainability technique that can be used 
with any type of ML algorithm, built on game theory, which works by 
calculating the contribution (Shapley value) of each feature (player) to 
the model ’ s final prediction (payout) ( Salih et al., 2025 ). Introduced by 
Lundberg and Lee (2017) , SHAP values provide a unified measure of 
feature importance by quantifying how much each feature changes a 
model ’ s prediction compared to the average prediction the model would 
make if it did not know any of the features. To better understand how 
SHAP values are calculated, the formal mathematical definition is rep -
resented by Eq. (7) :

sentiment based on the additive effect of explicit flaw-related vocabu -
lary, which simultaneously elevates the class 0 probability and maxi -

The local SHAP explanations presented demonstrate that the model 
classifies reviews by leveraging the most influential and task-relevant 
words, capturing key sentiment cues in the text. While the explain -
ability analysis above focuses on the clothing domain, a similar process 
was followed for the appliances and groceries domains. The overall 
patterns were mostly similar, but the impact of certain features differed 
across domains due to differences in vocabulary and contextualized 
review language.
4.5. Statistical validation
To ensure that the performance differences reported in Section 4.2
(Performance Comparison) and 4.3 (SHAP-Based Ensemble Base Model 
Ranking) were not due to random variation, statistical significance 
testing was conducted on three comparisons: StackFull vs. its base 
models, StackSHAP vs. its base models, and StackFull vs. the reduced 
SHAP-guided ensemble (StackSHAP). McNemar ’ s test (from Statsmodels 
v0.14.5 ), a non-parametric method for paired nominal data, was chosen. 
Originally introduced by McNemar in 1947, the test evaluates whether 
the marginal frequencies of two outcomes are equal ( Mukhtar et al., 
2017 ). In ML, it is commonly used to assess whether classifiers produce 
significantly different predictions on the same instances, focusing on 
disagreement cases ( Hasan et al., 2024 ). This approach is appropriate 
because all our models were evaluated on an identical held-out test set 
across the three domains.

5. Discussion
In this section, we are examining the practical implications of the 
experimental results, focusing on how model robustness and interpret -
ability influence decision-making in real business contexts, financial and 
operational risks associated with model errors. Technical preprocessing 
details are minimized to focus on actionable insights relevant to industry 
and applied research contexts.
5.1. Managerial and financial implications of domain-specific ensembles
Across appliances, groceries, and clothing, the proposed stacking 
ensembles consistently delivered strong performance, particularly in 
macro F1 and G-Mean, highlighting their superior ability to recognize 
minority classes, while the transformer-based ALBERT model achieved 
higher weighted F1, MCC, and Kappa scores, reflecting overall predic -
tive strength. Among the models, Extra Trees notably excelled in G- 
Mean, supporting balanced class recognition. The SHAP-guided Stack -
SHAP maintained performance comparable to StackFull with fewer base 
models, confirming that ensemble simplification does not compromise 
accuracy. These results demonstrate that the framework generalizes 
effectively across domains with varying linguistic patterns and data 
distributions. From a managerial perspective, such robust, domain- 
specific ensembles can reduce modeling costs by providing a single 
adaptive pipeline, while high minority-class sensitivity enables early 
detection or neutral or negative customer feedback, supporting proac -
tive interventions for reputational risk management and informed 
decision-making, consistent with financial literature linking sentiment 
analysis to market behaviour ( Deveikyte et al., 2022 ).
5.2. Translating interpretability into managerial action
The explainability component of the framework provides direct 
managerial value by transforming model outputs into actionable in -
sights across domains. By combining transparent learners such as Lo -
gistic Regression with SHAP-based interpretations for more complex 
models like XGBoost, the approach balances accuracy with interpretive 
depth, enabling both local and global understanding of sentiment 
drivers. At the instance level, SHAP highlights the specific terms that 
influence predictions, guiding product teams toward issues such as 
"leaks" in appliances or "shrinks" in clothing, informing design and 
supplier decisions, and supporting pricing or promotional adjustments 
when sentiment clusters around terms like "expensive." At the global 
level, SHAP aggregates feature and model importance to reveal consis -
tent predictors of sentiment, allowing analysts to track emerging con -
sumer concerns and prioritize interventions across supply chain 
management, marketing, and product improvements. In line with ( Bhatt 
et al., 2020 ), this interpretability ensures that sentiment insights remain 
transparent, auditable, and closely aligned with strategic business 
objectives.
5.3. Explainability trade-offs in ensemble architectures
The findings reveal clear trade-offs between predictive strength and 
interpretability within the ensemble designs. Although StackFull often 
achieves the highest marginal performance, its heterogeneous and more 
complex architecture reduces transparency and complicates practical 
deployment. StackSHAP, by contrast, delivers a more streamlined 
configuration that retains most of StackFull's accuracy while offering 
substantially better interpretability and computational efficiency. These 
results emphasize that model selection should reflect the requirements 
of the operational setting, as marginal gains in predictive power may not 
justify reduced explainability or increased maintenance demands,

5.4. Error analysis: The economic cost of misclassification and class 
imbalance
The error patterns across all domains show that class imbalance re -
mains the primary limitation of the framework, with minority (neutral) 
instances frequently misclassified despite strong overall performance. 
This is reflected in the G-Mean values, which indicate reduced sensi -
tivity to the minority class and highlight that statistical imbalance 
translates into meaningful managerial and economic risks. In practice, 
misclassifying neutral reviews, often early indicators of dissatisfaction 
or churn, carries substantially higher cost than small deviations among 
positive cases, underscoring the need for improved treatment of these 
economically sensitive errors. While preserving data authenticity was 
methodologically justified, the results suggest that more targeted stra -
tegies may be required to strengthen the minority class detection and 
ensure that sentiment signals used for managerial decision-making 
remain comprehensive and reliable.
6. Conclusion and future work
6.1. Summary of contributions
This study proposes an explainable stacking-based ensemble frame -
work for multi-domain, multiclass sentiment analysis on Amazon 
product reviews. Across appliances, groceries, and clothing, the en -
sembles consistently outperformed individual base learners on key class- 
sensitive metrics (weighted and macro F1, MCC, and Kappa scores), 
while the transformer benchmark ALBERT achieves the highest 
weighted F1, MCC, and Kappa scores overall. Importantly, the proposed 
ensembles yield superior macro F1 and G-Mean relative to ALBERT, 
indicating better minority-class recognition, and Extra Trees stands out 
among base models for G-Mean. A central contribution is the SHAP- 
based model-ranking mechanism, which identifies the most contribu -
tive base learners and enables a streamlined, more interpretable 
ensemble without sacrificing performance; combining global and local 
SHAP analysis with stacking enhances transparency in architectures 
often treated as black boxes.
6.2. Implications and applications
The findings offer practical implications for e-commerce platforms, 
manufacturers, and product managers seeking reliable sentiment- 
derived insights. Model outputs can be applied in review summariza -
tion, product quality monitoring, and personalized recommendation 
systems, where trustworthy sentiment predictions are essential. SHAP- 
based explanations further increase stakeholder confidence by clari -
fying the rationale behind sentiment assignments and helping detect 
domain-specific sentiment variations, while ensuring that the neutral 
class is not systematically overlooked.
6.3. Future work
Future work can extend this study in several directions. Moving from 
three-class sentiment to a fine-grained five-class formulation aligned 
with star ratings would allow more nuanced distinctions between " very 
poor" and " excellent " feedback. Incorporating stacked transformer-based 
models and sentiment-aware approaches such as ABSA, together with 
advanced topic modeling, could better capture product-specific con -
cerns. Multimodal extensions integrating text with product images 
would further reflect the complexity of real-world reviews. Low- 
resource product categories remain a key challenge, where techniques 
tailored to data scarcity may improve robustness. Finally, contrastive 
explanations (e.g., what-if review edits) could enrich interpretability by 
showing how small input changes affect predictions, offering actionable 
insights for manufacturers.
